## How to introduce it — a few principles · چند اصل برای معرفی

**English**

1. **Lead with the problem, not the tech.** Open on what the reader feels ("your messenger can read your chats"), then name Cipher. Specs come after.
2. **Show it.** Pin a short screen-recording or one clean screenshot — the floating bubble over a chat, or a before/after of plaintext → ciphertext. Visual posts travel much further.
3. **Stay modest.** For a security tool, restraint reads as trust. Claim only what's true; avoid "unbreakable," "untraceable," "100% safe."
4. **One link, once.** Put the site in the main post; keep the GitHub repo for a reply. Single-link posts reach more people.
5. **Protect your anonymity.** Post from the project account; keep your real name, photo, and personal handle out of it.
6. **Seed the first hour.** Ask a few friends to repost early, and reply to every question — early engagement is what spreads it.

**1/**

**3/**
> No account, no server, nothing to sign up for and nothing to leak. The code is open, so you don't have to take my word for any of it. It's a small tool. But if it lets one conversation happen that fear would have stopped, it was worth building.

## How to use it (shorts)

**The loop**
> How Cipher works, in three steps:
> 1. Open Cipher, type your message, set a password.
> 2. Copy the ciphertext and paste it into any chat.
> 3. Your friend pastes it back into Cipher with the same password and reads it.
> The password never travels through the app — share it in person.

**Install**
> To start: open cmos-jumper.github.io/Cipher and tap Install / Add to Home Screen — it runs offline afterward. On Android you can also install the APK from the repo's Releases. The same message sealed on one device opens on the other.

**Disguise**
> A quieter option: turn on a disguise mode, and your encrypted message can look like ordinary text or emoji instead of an obvious code — useful wherever a block of random characters would draw attention.

**Password habit**
> One habit worth keeping: send the password through a different channel than the message — ideally face to face. Cipher protects what you write, but it can't protect a password you paste into the same chat.
